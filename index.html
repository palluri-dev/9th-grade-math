Number Systems & Real Numbers

Every number you'll ever use in algebra lives somewhere in the real number system. This week you'll learn the map.

  • Classify numbers in the real number system

  • Evaluate expressions using PEMDAS

1.1

The Real Number System

▾
💡

Think of numbers like a family tree. Just like a person can be a child, a sibling, a student, and an athlete all at once, a number can belong to several categories at the same time.

The biggest category is Real Numbers — basically every number you can put on a number line. Inside that live two branches: Rational Numbers (numbers you can write as a fraction) and Irrational Numbers (numbers like π that go on forever without repeating). Inside Rational Numbers, you have Integers (…−3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3…), and inside those are Whole Numbers (no negatives), and inside those are Natural Numbers (counting numbers, starting at 1).

Quick test for rational vs irrational: If the decimal either stops (like 0.5) or repeats a pattern forever (like 0.333…), it's rational. If it goes on forever with no pattern (like π = 3.14159265…), it's irrational.

The nested structure (each set lives inside the one above it):

Properties of Real Numbers

▾
💡

Properties are rules that numbers always follow — no exceptions, ever. Think of them like traffic laws for math. You don't have to memorize them as new ideas — you already USE them every day without knowing their names.

For example: when you say "5 plus 3 is the same as 3 plus 5" — that's the Commutative Property. When you say "3 groups of (4 + 2) is the same as 3×4 plus 3×2" — that's the Distributive Property. These properties let us rearrange and simplify expressions in algebra, which is why we name them.

The most powerful one is Distributive — you'll use it constantly in algebra to expand or simplify expressions like 3(x + 5) = 3x + 15.

Order of Operations (PEMDAS)

▾
💡

Imagine you're following a recipe. If you bake the cake before mixing the batter, it comes out wrong. Math expressions are the same — do the operations in the WRONG order and you get the wrong answer.

PEMDAS tells you the correct order: Parentheses first (work inside them), then Exponents, then Multiplication and Division (left to right, whichever comes first), then Addition and Subtraction (left to right).

The most common mistake: Students think multiplication always comes before division, or addition before subtraction. Wrong! M and D are equal priority — do whichever comes first reading left to right. Same for A and S.

Example mistake: 12 ÷ 4 × 3. Don't do 4×3=12 first! Read left to right: 12÷4=3, then 3×3=9. ✓

PEMDAS Order: 1. Parentheses ( ) [ ] { } — work from innermost outward 2. Exponents — powers and roots 3. Multiplication & Division — LEFT TO RIGHT (equal priority!) 4. Addition & Subtraction — LEFT TO RIGHT (equal priority!)
Common mistake: 12 ÷ 4 × 3 ≠ 12 ÷ 12 = 1. The correct answer: (12÷4)×3 = 3×3 = 9. Always go left to right for M/D and A/S.

Absolute Value

▾
💡

Think of absolute value like GPS distance. If you're standing at the mall (position 0) and walk 5 stores to the right, you're 5 stores away. If you walk 5 stores to the LEFT, you're still 5 stores away. Distance is always positive — it doesn't matter which direction you went.

That's exactly what absolute value does: it gives you the distance from zero, stripping away any negative sign. |−8| = 8 because −8 is 8 steps away from zero. |8| = 8 for the same reason.

Key insight: The absolute value bars | | act like parentheses — solve everything inside them first, THEN take the absolute value.

|x| = x if x is positive or zero → |5| = 5 |x| = −x if x is negative → |−5| = −(−5) = 5 The result is ALWAYS ≥ 0
Absolute value equations have TWO solutions: |x| = 7 means x = 7 OR x = −7, because both 7 and −7 are exactly 7 steps from zero.
P1  Evaluate: |−8| + |3| − |−2|Show Answer
Answer: 9
8 + 3 − 2 = 9
P2  Solve: |x| = 7Show Answer
x = 7 or x = −7
Both 7 and −7 are exactly 7 units from zero on the number line.
P3  Simplify: |3 − 10| + |−4 + 1|Show Answer
Answer: 10
Work inside first: |−7| + |−3| = 7 + 3 = 10
P4  Can |x| ever be negative? Explain.Show Answer
Never
No. Absolute value measures distance, and distance is always 0 or positive. |x| ≥ 0 for every real number x.

Learning Objectives

2.1

Solving Linear Equations

▾
💡

Think of an equation like a balance scale. The equals sign (=) is the pivot point in the middle. Whatever weight is on the left MUST equal the weight on the right. Your job is to get x by itself on one side.

The golden rule: whatever you do to one side, you MUST do to the other side — otherwise you break the balance. Add 5 to the left? Add 5 to the right. Divide the left by 3? Divide the right by 3.

Work in reverse order of PEMDAS: undo addition/subtraction first, then multiplication/division. Always check your answer by plugging it back into the original equation.

Two special cases: If you simplify and get something like "3 = 7" (never true), there's no solution. If you get "5 = 5" (always true), every number is a solution.

Step-by-step process:
1. Distribute (remove parentheses)   2. Combine like terms on each side   3. Move all x terms to one side   4. Move all numbers to the other side   5. Divide to isolate x   6. Check!

Special cases to recognize:

  • No solution: x disappears and you get a FALSE statement like −6 = 5
  • Infinite solutions: x disappears and you get a TRUE statement like 12 = 12

Literal Equations

▾
💡

A literal equation is just a formula with multiple letters. You've seen these before — like the area formula A = l × w, or the distance formula d = r × t. A "literal equation" just means you have to rearrange the formula to solve for a different letter.

For example, d = rt solves for d. But what if you want to find t? Just treat every other letter (d and r) as if they were regular numbers, and solve for t the same way you always solve equations. So: d = rt → divide both sides by r → t = d/r.

The technique is identical to regular equations. The only difference is that your "answer" will have letters in it instead of just numbers. Don't let that scare you — just focus on isolating the target variable.

Key technique: Treat every letter you are NOT solving for as if it were a number. Then use the same inverse operations (add/subtract to move terms, multiply/divide to isolate) that you use with regular equations.

Linear Inequalities

▾
💡

Instead of "equals", inequalities say one side is bigger or smaller. Real life is full of inequalities: "You must be at least 16 to drive" means age ≥ 16 — not exactly 16, but 16 or more. "The elevator holds fewer than 10 people" means people < 10.

Solving inequalities works EXACTLY like solving equations — same steps, same inverse operations. There's only ONE extra rule to remember: if you multiply or divide BOTH sides by a negative number, you must FLIP the inequality sign.

Why flip? Think about 2 < 6. If you multiply both sides by −1: −2 and −6. Now −2 > −6 (−2 is closer to zero, so it's bigger). The relationship flipped!

Graphing: Open circle ○ means the boundary point is NOT included (< or >). Closed/filled circle ● means it IS included (≤ or ≥). The arrow shows all solutions.

THE flip rule: −2x < 8 → divide by −2 → x > −4. The sign FLIPPED from < to >. Forget this and you'll get wrong answers every time.

Compound Inequalities & Absolute Value Equations

▾
💡

Compound inequalities have TWO conditions at once. Like an amusement park ride that says "You must be at least 48 inches tall but no more than 78 inches tall." That's: 48 ≤ height ≤ 78. You solve it by doing the same operation to ALL three parts at once.

Absolute value equations are interesting because they have TWO answers. |x| = 5 means: what numbers are exactly 5 steps from zero? Both 5 AND −5. So you split into two cases and solve each separately.

For absolute value inequalities, there's a pattern: |x| < c creates a "between" situation (AND), while |x| > c creates an "outside" situation (OR). Think: less than = locking in between the fences. Greater than = escaping outside both fences.

Compound AND: 2 < x + 3 < 8 → solve all three parts together Absolute value equation: |ax+b| = c → Case 1: ax+b = c → Case 2: ax+b = −c Absolute value inequality: |x| < c → −c < x < c (between the fences — AND) |x| > c → x < −c OR x > c (outside both fences — OR)

Functions & Relations

Functions are the language algebra uses to describe how one quantity depends on another.

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish relations from functions
  • Find domain and range
  • Use and evaluate function notation f(x)
  • Compose two functions
3.1

Relations vs. Functions

▾
💡

A relation is any pairing of inputs and outputs. Think of a vending machine: you press a button (input), and you get a snack (output). A function is a special, reliable vending machine — press button B3, you ALWAYS get the same thing. A broken machine that sometimes gives chips and sometimes gives nothing when you press B3 is a relation but NOT a function.

In math terms: a function means each x-value (input) gives exactly ONE y-value (output). The same input cannot give two different outputs.

Vertical Line Test: Look at a graph. If you can draw any vertical line that crosses the graph MORE than once — it's not a function. Why? Because that x-value would give two different y-values. A circle, for example, fails this test.

Important: Two different inputs CAN give the same output — that's fine! Many students confuse this. In {(1,3),(2,3),(5,3)}, all inputs give output 3. That's a valid function.

Vertical Line Test: Draw any vertical line on the graph. If it crosses the curve at more than one point → NOT a function. Passes? → Function.

P2  Function? {(2,5),(4,5),(6,5)}Show Answer

3.2

Domain and Range

▾
💡

Domain = all the inputs you're ALLOWED to use. Range = all the outputs that actually come out.

Imagine you run a lemonade stand. The domain is all the orders you can accept — you can only take orders for amounts you can actually make. The range is all the cups you actually pour.

Two things restrict domain: (1) You can't divide by zero — so if x appears in a denominator, set it ≠ 0 and exclude those x-values. (2) You can't take the square root of a negative number (in real numbers) — so if x is under a square root, it must be ≥ 0.

Interval notation: [ means "include this endpoint" (≤ or ≥). ( means "exclude it" (< or >). So [2, ∞) means x ≥ 2, and (−∞, 3) means x < 3. Use ∞ with parentheses — you can never "reach" infinity.

Domain restrictions: Fractions: denominator ≠ 0 Even roots: radicand ≥ 0 Interval notation: [a, b] = a ≤ x ≤ b (both included) (a, b) = a < x < b (both excluded) [a, ∞) = x ≥ a (∞ always uses parenthesis)

3.3

Function Notation & Evaluation

▾
💡

f(x) is just a fancy way of writing y. "f(x)" is read "f of x" — it means "the output of the function f when the input is x." So f(3) means "plug in 3 wherever you see x and calculate the output."

Think of f like a machine or recipe: f(x) = 2x + 1 is a machine that takes any number, doubles it, and adds 1. f(5) = 2(5)+1 = 11. f(−3) = 2(−3)+1 = −5.

Composition f(g(x)) means "run the inner function first, then feed its result into the outer function." Like a car wash with two stages: first the soap (g), then the rinse (f). You can't rinse first.

Evaluating: f(a) = substitute a for every x f(x) = 3x − 2 → f(4) = 3(4) − 2 = 10 Composition: (f ∘ g)(x) = f(g(x)) Step 1: Evaluate g(x) first Step 2: Use that result as the input for f

Slope

▾

Slope is how steep a line is. Think of hiking: a steep mountain trail has a high slope. A flat road has a slope of zero. You measure slope as "rise over run" — for every step you take horizontally (run), how many steps do you go up or down (rise)?

A positive slope means the line goes up as you move right (like climbing a hill). Negative slope means it goes down (like skiing downhill). Zero slope is perfectly flat. Undefined slope is a vertical cliff — straight up and down.

In real life, slope = rate of change. If a car drives and you graph distance vs. time, the slope IS the speed. If you earn $12/hour and graph money vs. hours worked, slope = $12. Slope always tells you "how much does y change per unit change in x?"

▾
💡

Perpendicular lines make a perfect 90° corner. Their slopes are "negative reciprocals" of each other. To find a perpendicular slope: flip the fraction upside down and change the sign. Slope of 2? Perpendicular slope = −1/2. Slope of −3/4? Perpendicular = +4/3.

Quick check: Multiply the two slopes together. If you get −1, they're perpendicular. If you get the same number, they're parallel (if y-intercepts differ).

5.1

Types of Systems & Graphing Method

▾
💡

A system of equations asks: where do two lines meet? Each equation draws a line on a graph. The solution is the point where both lines cross — that point satisfies BOTH equations at the same time.

Three things can happen: (1) The lines cross at ONE point — one solution. (2) The lines are parallel and never cross — no solution, like trying to find where two train tracks that never meet intersect. (3) The lines are the EXACT same line — infinite solutions, every single point on the line works for both.

You can spot which case before graphing just by looking at the slopes and y-intercepts. Same slope, different y-intercept? Parallel → no solution. Same slope AND same y-intercept? Same line → infinite solutions. Different slopes? They'll cross somewhere → one solution.

Best used when: one variable is already alone on one side (y = ... or x = ...), or when it's easy to isolate one variable quickly.

  • Solve one equation for one variable (if not already done)
  • Substitute that expression into the OTHER equation
  • Solve the resulting single-variable equation
  • Back-substitute to find the other variable
  • Check both values in BOTH original equations

Elimination Method

▾
💡

Elimination is "add and destroy." The goal is to make one variable disappear (get eliminated) when you add the two equations together. For that to work, one variable needs opposite coefficients in the two equations — like +3y and −3y. When you add those, 3y + (−3y) = 0, and y is gone!

If the coefficients aren't opposite, you multiply one or both equations by numbers to MAKE them opposite. For example, if one equation has 2x and another has 3x, multiply the first by 3 and the second by −2 to get 6x and −6x — then they cancel when added.

Best used when: both equations are in Ax + By = C form with no variable already isolated, or when substitution would create annoying fractions.

  • Write both equations in Ax + By = C form
  • Multiply one or both equations so one variable has opposite coefficients
  • Add the equations — one variable cancels out
  • Solve for the remaining variable
  • Back-substitute into either original equation

6.1

Laws of Exponents

▾
💡

Exponents are just a shortcut for repeated multiplication. x³ means x × x × x. x⁵ means x × x × x × x × x. So x³ × x⁵ should be x × x × x × x × x × x × x × x = x⁸. That's where the Product Rule (add the exponents) comes from — you're just counting how many x's you're multiplying together.

The other rules all come from the same logic. Dividing x⁷ by x³ is like cancelling three x's from the top and bottom, leaving x⁴ — that's the Quotient Rule (subtract exponents).

The two tricky ones: x⁰ = 1 (any non-zero number to the zero power is 1 — you can prove it: x³/x³ = 1, but the quotient rule gives x³⁻³ = x⁰, so x⁰ must equal 1). And x⁻² = 1/x² — a negative exponent means "put it in the denominator."

▾
💡

A polynomial is just an expression made of terms — numbers, variables, or both multiplied together, all added or subtracted. Like 3x² − 2x + 5. That's three terms: 3x², −2x, and 5. Poly means "many," nomial means "terms."

Adding polynomials is just combining like terms — terms with the same variable AND the same exponent. Think of it like sorting laundry: x² terms go with x² terms, x terms with x terms, and numbers with numbers. You can ONLY combine matching types.

Subtraction trick: When subtracting a polynomial, you're subtracting EVERY term inside. Distribute the negative sign to each term first: (5x²+3x−1) − (2x²−x+4) becomes 5x²+3x−1 −2x²+x−4. Notice: subtracting −x becomes +x, subtracting +4 becomes −4.

Subtraction mistake to avoid: (5x²+3x−1) − (2x²−x+4). The minus sign applies to ALL terms of the second polynomial. Don't just subtract the first term!

Multiplying Polynomials

▾
💡

Multiplying polynomials is just the distributive property used repeatedly. Every term in the first polynomial must multiply every term in the second. For two binomials (two terms each), there are 4 pairings — that's FOIL: First, Outer, Inner, Last.

Think of it like a double-order: if you order (a burger OR fries) AND (a coke OR juice), you have 4 combinations: burger+coke, burger+juice, fries+coke, fries+juice. FOIL just names those four pairings for (x+3)(x−5).

Special patterns are shortcuts worth memorizing. (a+b)² is NOT a²+b². It's a²+2ab+b² — don't forget the middle term! This is one of the most common mistakes in algebra. (a+b)(a−b) is elegant: the middle terms cancel, leaving just a²−b².

FOIL for (a+b)(c+d): F: a·c (First terms) O: a·d (Outer terms) I: b·c (Inner terms) L: b·d (Last terms) Then combine like terms. Special Products — MEMORIZE THESE: (a+b)² = a² + 2ab + b² ← middle term is 2ab, NOT 0! (a−b)² = a² − 2ab + b² (a+b)(a−b) = a² − b² ← middle terms cancel out

7.1

GCF Factoring

▾
💡

Factoring out the GCF is like reverse-distributing. Look at 12x³−8x²+4x. Every term is divisible by 4x. So you can "un-distribute" the 4x: pull it out front and divide each term by it. Result: 4x(3x²−2x+1).

To find the GCF: (1) Find the largest number that divides all the coefficients evenly. (2) Find the lowest power of each variable that appears in ALL terms. Multiply those together — that's your GCF.

Always check GCF first before trying any other factoring method. It simplifies everything and is the fastest step. After factoring out the GCF, look at what's left in the parentheses — it might factor further.

  • Find the GCF of all coefficients (largest number dividing all evenly)
  • Find the lowest power of each variable appearing in every term
  • Write GCF × (each term ÷ GCF)
  • Check by distributing back — you should get the original expression

Solving Quadratic Equations

▾
💡

A quadratic equation (ax²+bx+c=0) can have 0, 1, or 2 solutions. This is different from linear equations which always have exactly one. The solutions are the x-values where the parabola (U-shaped curve) crosses the x-axis.

Method 1 — Factoring: If you can factor the left side, set each factor = 0 and solve. This works because of the Zero Product Property: if A×B=0, then either A=0 or B=0 (or both).

Method 2 — Square Root: When there's no middle term (x² = k), just take the square root of both sides. Remember ±√k — both positive and negative square roots work.

Method 3 — Quadratic Formula: ALWAYS works, no matter what. Memorize it like a song: x equals negative b, plus or minus the square root of b-squared minus 4ac, all over 2a. The part under the radical (b²−4ac) is called the discriminant — it tells you how many solutions exist before you even calculate them.

Quadratic Formula (always works): x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) / 2a Discriminant D = b²−4ac tells you: D > 0 → TWO real solutions (crosses x-axis twice) D = 0 → ONE real solution (touches x-axis once) D < 0 → NO real solutions (parabola misses x-axis entirely)

8.1

Measures of Central Tendency & Spread

▾
💡

Measures of center describe a "typical" value. Measures of spread describe how scattered the data is. Imagine asking "how tall is a typical 9th grader?" The center gives you one representative number. But two classes could have the same average height with very different spreads — one class where everyone is near the average, and another where heights range wildly.

Mean vs Median — which to use? If a celebrity earns $50 million and joins a group of 9 people earning $40,000 each, the mean salary jumps to about $5 million — totally unrepresentative! The median stays near $40,000. So: use median when there are extreme values (outliers). Use mean when data is evenly spread.

IQR (Interquartile Range) is the spread of the middle 50% of data. It ignores the top 25% and bottom 25%, making it resistant to outliers. Q1 is the median of the bottom half. Q3 is the median of the top half. IQR = Q3 − Q1.

Outlier detection formula: A value is an outlier if it falls below Q1−1.5(IQR) or above Q3+1.5(IQR). These are called "fences."

8.2

Box Plots & Five-Number Summary

▾
💡

A box plot is a visual summary of data using 5 key numbers. Imagine ranking all students in your class by height and dividing them into four equal groups. The box in the box plot shows where the middle two groups (the middle 50%) are. The whiskers extend to the smallest and largest values that aren't outliers.

Reading a box plot tells you: Where is the center? (median line inside the box). How spread is the middle half? (width of the box = IQR). Are the data skewed? (if one whisker is much longer than the other, data is stretched in that direction). Are there outliers? (dots plotted beyond the whiskers).

The five-number summary: Minimum, Q1, Median (Q2), Q3, Maximum. These five numbers completely describe the distribution of a dataset — no other statistics needed for a box plot.

Five-Number Summary: Min | Q1 | Median | Q3 | Max Box Plot Visual: |-----[ | ]-----| * (outlier) Min Q1 Med Q3 Max Outlier rule: Lower fence = Q1 − 1.5 × IQR Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5 × IQR Any value OUTSIDE these fences is an outlier (shown as a dot ✱)

8.3

Scatter Plots & Correlation

▾
💡

A scatter plot shows whether two things tend to go together. Each data point is a pair (x, y) plotted on a graph. If the dots form a pattern that goes upward left-to-right, there's a positive correlation — as x increases, y tends to increase too (like hours studied and test scores). Downward pattern = negative correlation (like hours of TV and GPA). No pattern = no correlation.

Correlation strength tells you how tight the pattern is. Strong correlation = points are close to a line. Weak correlation = points are scattered loosely. Perfect correlation would mean all points fall exactly on a line (rare in real life).

The most important concept: CORRELATION ≠ CAUSATION. Just because two things correlate doesn't mean one causes the other. Ice cream sales and drowning deaths both rise in summer — but ice cream doesn't cause drowning. A third factor (hot weather) causes both. Always think critically about what might explain a correlation.

8.4

Line of Best Fit & Linear Regression

▾
💡

A line of best fit is a single straight line drawn through a scatter plot to summarize the trend. It won't pass through every point, but it passes as close to all of them as possible — roughly equal numbers of points above and below it.

Once you have the equation of the line, you can use it like a crystal ball: plug in an x-value to predict a y-value. Interpolation (predicting within the range of your data) is pretty reliable. Extrapolation (predicting way beyond your data) is risky — trends don't always continue forever.

Interpreting slope and y-intercept in context matters a lot. If your equation is y = 6.8x + 48.2 where x is study hours and y is test score: slope (6.8) means each extra hour of studying predicts 6.8 more points. Y-intercept (48.2) predicts the score with zero study time. Always connect numbers to their real-world meaning.

Properties of a good line of best fit:
1. Passes through or near the "middle" of the cloud of points
2. Roughly equal number of points above and below
3. Passes through the point (x̄, ȳ) — the means of both variables

📐 Formula Sheet

Lines & Slope

  • Slope: m = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁)
  • Slope-Intercept: y = mx + b
  • Point-Slope: y−y₁ = m(x−x₁)
  • Standard: Ax + By = C
  • Parallel: m₁ = m₂
  • Perpendicular: m₁ × m₂ = −1

Quadratics

  • Standard: ax²+bx+c = 0
  • Formula: x=(−b±√(b²−4ac))/2a
  • Discriminant: D = b²−4ac
  • D>0 → 2 real solutions
  • D=0 → 1 real solution
  • D<0 → no real solutions

Exponents

  • aᵐ·aⁿ = aᵐ⁺ⁿ (product)
  • aᵐ/aⁿ = aᵐ⁻ⁿ (quotient)
  • (aᵐ)ⁿ = aᵐⁿ (power)
  • (ab)ⁿ = aⁿbⁿ
  • a⁰ = 1
  • a⁻ⁿ = 1/aⁿ

Special Products

  • (a+b)² = a²+2ab+b²
  • (a−b)² = a²−2ab+b²
  • (a+b)(a−b) = a²−b²

Statistics

  • Mean: x̄ = Σx/n
  • IQR = Q3−Q1
  • Lower fence: Q1−1.5(IQR)
  • Upper fence: Q3+1.5(IQR)

Properties

  • Commutative: a+b=b+a
  • Associative: (a+b)+c=a+(b+c)
  • Distributive: a(b+c)=ab+ac
  • Identity: a+0=a; a×1=a
  • Zero Product: ab=0 → a=0 or b=0
